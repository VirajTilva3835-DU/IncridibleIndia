Qutub Minar, Delhi

"A 12th-century marvel of Indo-Islamic architecture — a monument that reaches for the skies."

About the Qutub Minar

The Qutub Minar is a towering minaret located in the Mehrauli area of Delhi. It was commissioned by Qutb-ud-din Aibak in 1192 and completed by his successor Iltutmish. Standing at a height of 73 meters (240 feet), it is the tallest brick minaret in the world and a fine example of Indo-Islamic architecture.


The structure was built to celebrate the victory of Muslim rule in India and serves as a part of the Qutub Complex, which includes the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque and the famous Iron Pillar. In 1993, it was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its historical and architectural significance.


Interesting Facts

  • Qutub Minar has five distinct storeys, each marked by a projecting balcony.
  • The first three storeys are made of red sandstone, while the top two are of marble and sandstone.
  • The Iron Pillar in the Qutub Complex has not rusted for over 1,600 years.
  • Qutub Minar slightly leans by about 65 cm from the vertical axis.
  • It inspired several minarets built later in South Asia.

Travel Tips & Nearby Attractions

  • Best visited early morning or during sunset for golden light views.
  • Closed on Mondays; open from 7:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • Climbing inside the minar is restricted for safety reasons.
  • Nearby attractions: Mehrauli Archaeological Park, Lotus Temple, Hauz Khas Village, and Humayun’s Tomb.
